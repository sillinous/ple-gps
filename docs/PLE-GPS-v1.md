# Post-Labor Economics — Governance Process Standard (PLE-GPS)
## Adapted from APQC Process Classification Framework v7.3

**Version:** 1.0 — Foundation Draft
**Framework Origin:** APQC PCF Cross-Industry v7.3
**Adapted By:** The Foundation for Global Progress
**Purpose:** A universal process taxonomy for designing, operating, and improving
governance institutions in a post-labor economic context.

---

## Preamble: Why a Process Standard for Post-Labor Governance?

The transition from labor-centric to post-labor economies does not eliminate the
need for institutions. It transforms what institutions *do* — and who (or what)
*does it*. Every organized human community still requires:

- Direction-setting and collective decision-making
- Creation and stewardship of shared value
- Distribution of resources and access
- Delivery of services and public goods
- Support for community members
- Management of shared infrastructure
- Stewardship of collective resources
- Protection from risk and harm
- Relationships between institutions
- Continuous improvement of all of the above

These functions are universal. They existed before capitalism, they exist within
it, and they will persist beyond it. What changes in a post-labor context is the
*performer allocation* (human vs. AI vs. hybrid), the *value model* (commons-based
vs. profit-based), and the *governance mechanism* (participatory vs. hierarchical).

The APQC Process Classification Framework encodes the world's most comprehensive
taxonomy of organizational work — ~1,000 processes at 5 levels of granularity,
with cross-process integration logic, maturity models, and measurement frameworks.
By translating this taxonomy into post-labor governance language, we create:

1. **A complete operational checklist** for any PLE institution
2. **A shared language** for cross-institutional coordination
3. **A maturity model** for institutional development
4. **A work allocation framework** for human-AI task distribution
5. **An integration standard** for how PLE institutions connect to each other

This is not theory. This is infrastructure.

### Translation Key: Enterprise → Post-Labor

| Enterprise Concept | PLE Equivalent | Why It Changes |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | Resource inflows (automated production, taxes, commons yield) | Profit motive replaced by stewardship mandate |
| Customers | Community members, citizens, participants | Relationship is civic, not transactional |
| Employees | Contributors, stewards, participants | "Employment" is no longer the organizing principle |
| Products | Public goods, shared services, commons | Created for access, not sale |
| Market share | Community coverage, access equity | Success = universal access, not competitive capture |
| Shareholders | Stakeholders, all community members | Governance is participatory, not ownership-based |
| Competitive advantage | Institutional effectiveness | No competition for profit; coordination for impact |
| HR | Human Development | People aren't "resources" to be "managed" |
| Sales | Access facilitation, enrollment, matching | No persuasion needed; matching needs to services |
| ROI | Impact-to-resource ratio | Returns measured in human outcomes, not financial |

## CATEGORY 1.0 — Develop Collective Vision and Governance Strategy

**Domain:** Civic Operating Process | **Value Type:** Directional
**PCF Origin:** Category 1.0 — Develop Vision and Strategy

**What Changes in PLE Context:**
Strategy in a post-labor institution is not set by executives and cascaded down.
It emerges from participatory processes, informed by collective intelligence,
and ratified through democratic governance. The "business model" becomes a
"value creation and distribution model" where the question is not "how do we
capture value?" but "how do we create and distribute value equitably?"

---

### 1.1 Define Collective Vision and Long-Term Direction

**Purpose:** Establish what the institution exists to do, who it serves, and what
community it aspires to build over a multi-generational horizon.

#### L3 Processes
```
1.1.1  Assess the external environment (ecological, technological, social)
1.1.2  Survey the institutional landscape (peer institutions, gaps, overlaps)
1.1.3  Identify and synthesize community needs and aspirations
1.1.4  Evaluate existing institutional capabilities
1.1.5  Establish collective vision through participatory process
1.1.6  Communicate and embed the vision across the community
```

#### L4 Activities
```
1.1.1.1  Monitor ecological and climate indicators
1.1.1.2  Track technology disruption patterns (AI, automation, energy)
1.1.1.3  Assess demographic and social shifts
1.1.1.4  Monitor global post-labor transition indicators
1.1.2.1  Map peer institutions and their coverage
1.1.2.2  Identify institutional gaps in community service
1.1.3.1  Conduct community assemblies and listening sessions
1.1.3.2  Operate continuous feedback channels (digital + physical)
1.1.3.3  Synthesize community input into needs taxonomy
1.1.4.1  Inventory institutional capabilities and assets
1.1.4.2  Assess human and AI capacity
1.1.5.1  Facilitate vision workshops with representative participation
1.1.5.2  Draft and iterate vision through public comment
1.1.5.3  Ratify vision through democratic mechanism
1.1.6.1  Build multilingual, multi-format communication plan
1.1.6.2  Embed vision into all institutional touchpoints
```

#### L5 Tasks (sample)
```
1.1.1.4.1  Subscribe to automation displacement indicators by region
1.1.1.4.2  Produce quarterly "State of the Transition" briefing
1.1.3.1.1  Schedule and facilitate monthly community assemblies
1.1.3.1.2  Ensure representation across demographics and geographies
1.1.5.1.1  Design inclusive workshop formats (accessible, multilingual)
```

#### KPIs
- Vision awareness (% community members who can articulate the vision)
- Participation rate in vision-setting processes
- Vision-strategy alignment (% initiatives traceable to vision)
- Community satisfaction with institutional direction (survey)
- Representation equity in vision-setting (demographic parity)

#### Integration Triggers
- Output → 1.2 (vision anchors governance strategy)
- Output → 16.0 (vision informs democratic structures)
- Receives ← 3.1 (community intelligence informs vision)
- Receives ← 6.4 (community satisfaction informs vision refresh)
- Receives ← 13.7 (institutional performance data informs vision)
- Receives ← 15.0 (flourishing indicators inform what "good" looks like)

### 1.2 Develop Governance Strategy

**Purpose:** Translate the collective vision into a concrete, time-bound strategy
for institutional action — choices about what to prioritize, what resources to
allocate, and what to explicitly *not* do.

#### L3 Processes
```
1.2.1  Develop institutional mission and mandate
1.2.2  Evaluate strategic options through participatory analysis
1.2.3  Formulate domain-specific strategies (by community need area)
1.2.4  Develop inter-institutional coordination strategies
1.2.5  Align functional strategies (technology, human development, resources)
1.2.6  Design and sequence transformation initiatives
1.2.7  Translate strategy into measures, targets, and accountability
```

#### L4 Activities
```
1.2.2.1  Generate strategic options with community input
1.2.2.2  Score options against equity, sustainability, feasibility
1.2.2.3  Conduct participatory prioritization (ranked choice, deliberation)
1.2.3.1  Develop strategy per community need domain (health, education,
         housing, nutrition, culture, environment, etc.)
1.2.5.1  Align technology strategy (feeds 8.1)
1.2.5.2  Align human development strategy (feeds 7.1)
1.2.5.3  Align resource strategy (feeds 9.1)
1.2.5.4  Align AI/agent allocation strategy (feeds 14.0)
1.2.7.1  Build institutional impact scorecard (not financial BSC)
1.2.7.2  Set annual targets per strategic theme
1.2.7.3  Publish transparent strategy dashboard accessible to all
```

#### KPIs
- Strategy plan completion rate (% initiatives on track)
- Resource-strategy alignment (% resources tied to strategic priorities)
- Strategy cascade depth (% of institution with aligned objectives)
- Community confidence in institutional strategy (survey)
- Strategy refresh cycle time
- Equity impact score (does strategy reduce or increase disparities?)

#### Integration Triggers
- Output → ALL civic operating categories (resource allocation, priorities)
- Output → 9.1 (resource plan targets)
- Output → 7.1 (human development planning)
- Output → 8.1 (technology roadmap)
- Output → 14.0 (AI governance alignment)
- Output → 13.4 (transformation initiatives)
- Receives ← 11.1 (risk landscape shapes strategy)
- Receives ← 12.0 (inter-institutional expectations)
- Receives ← 16.0 (democratic mandates constrain/direct strategy)

### 1.3 Manage Strategic Initiatives

**Purpose:** Execute strategy through a managed portfolio of cross-functional
initiatives with transparent governance, community visibility, and adaptive management.

#### L3 Processes
```
1.3.1  Develop strategic initiative roadmap
1.3.2  Govern strategic initiative portfolio (publicly transparent)
1.3.3  Track and report initiative progress to community
1.3.4  Adapt strategy based on outcomes and community feedback
```

#### L4 Activities
```
1.3.1.1  Identify and define strategic initiatives with community input
1.3.1.2  Sequence initiatives by dependency, equity impact, and feasibility
1.3.1.3  Assign stewardship (not "ownership" — stewardship implies duty)
1.3.1.4  Allocate resources to initiatives through transparent process
1.3.2.1  Establish governance cadence with public reporting
1.3.2.2  Review initiative health quarterly with community observers
1.3.2.3  Make continue/pause/redirect decisions with documented rationale
1.3.3.1  Publish real-time initiative dashboard
1.3.3.2  Produce plain-language progress reports for community
1.3.4.1  Assess strategy-reality gaps with outcome data
1.3.4.2  Revise strategy with community input when gaps emerge
```

### 1.4 Design and Validate Value Creation Models

**Purpose:** Explicitly design how the institution creates, delivers, and
distributes value — the logic connecting strategy to community outcomes.

#### L3 Processes
```
1.4.1  Design the community value proposition
1.4.2  Design the resource model (inflows and allocation)
1.4.3  Design the operating model (how work gets done, by whom/what)
1.4.4  Validate and iterate the institutional model
```

#### L4 Activities
```
1.4.1.1  Define community segments and needs (not "customer segments")
1.4.1.2  Articulate value proposition per community need area
1.4.2.1  Define resource inflow model (automated production yields, taxes,
         commons revenue, grants, inter-institutional transfers)
1.4.2.2  Define resource allocation model (needs-based, universal, hybrid)
1.4.3.1  Map key activities, capabilities, and partnerships
1.4.3.2  Define human-AI work allocation model
1.4.3.3  Define cost/resource structure
1.4.4.1  Test institutional model assumptions against community outcomes
1.4.4.2  Iterate model based on equity and effectiveness data
```

#### Integration Triggers
- Output → 2.0 (value proposition guides public goods design)
- Output → 3.0 (community engagement aligned to value model)
- Output → 9.1 (resource model anchors financial planning)
- Output → 14.0 (operating model defines AI allocation)
- Receives ← 3.1, 6.3 (community feedback validates/challenges model)
- Receives ← 15.0 (flourishing data validates whether model works)

## CATEGORY 2.0 — Design and Steward Public Goods and Services

**Domain:** Civic Operating Process | **Value Type:** Shared Value Creation
**PCF Origin:** Category 2.0 — Develop and Manage Products and Services

**What Changes in PLE Context:**
"Products" become public goods. The development lifecycle persists — conceive,
design, test, deploy, manage lifecycle — but the design criteria shift from
market viability to community impact, universal access, and sustainability.
There is no "product-market fit" — there is "service-community fit."

---

#### L3 Processes
```
2.1.1  Define public goods development governance
2.1.2  Manage public goods portfolio
2.1.3  Develop and manage public goods roadmap
2.1.4  Manage development lifecycle stage gates
```

#### L4 Activities
```
2.1.1.1  Establish development governance with community representation
2.1.1.2  Define stage-gate criteria (equity, sustainability, accessibility)
2.1.2.1  Inventory all active development initiatives
2.1.2.2  Prioritize by community impact, equity, and feasibility
2.1.2.3  Allocate development resources across portfolio
2.1.3.1  Map public goods roadmap by quarter/year
2.1.3.2  Publish roadmap for community visibility and input
```

---

### 2.2 Identify and Define New Public Goods

#### L3 Processes
```
2.2.1  Research community needs and capability gaps
2.2.2  Translate community needs into service requirements
2.2.3  Identify innovations from peer institutions and technology
2.2.4  Define and prioritize new public good requirements
2.2.5  Evaluate and select concepts for development
```

#### L4 Activities
```
2.2.1.1  Conduct community needs assessment (VoC → Voice of Community)
2.2.1.2  Analyze peer institution public goods (what exists, what's missing)
2.2.1.3  Monitor emerging technology capabilities
```

## CATEGORY 3.0 — Facilitate Access and Community Engagement

**Domain:** Civic Operating Process | **Value Type:** Connection and Access
**PCF Origin:** Category 3.0 — Market and Sell Products and Services

**What Changes in PLE Context:**
There is nothing to "sell." The function transforms from persuasion and revenue
generation to *awareness, access facilitation, and need-matching.* The underlying
processes persist: understand community needs (market research → community
intelligence), develop engagement strategy (marketing strategy → engagement
strategy), and facilitate connections between people and services (selling →
access facilitation and enrollment).

---

## CATEGORY 7.0 — Develop Human Potential and Civic Participation

**Domain:** Institutional Support | **Value Type:** Human Capability
**PCF Origin:** Category 7.0 — Develop and Manage Human Capital

**THIS IS THE MOST TRANSFORMED CATEGORY.**

In a post-labor world, "Human Resources" — the management of people as production
inputs — is obsolete. What replaces it is *Human Development* — the cultivation
of human potential, purpose, and civic participation. People are not "capital"
to be managed. They are the *reason the institution exists*.

The sub-processes transform dramatically:

| Enterprise HR | PLE Human Development |
|---|---|
| Recruit employees | Invite and activate community contributors |
| Manage performance | Support contribution and growth |
| Compensate labor | Ensure universal resource access + recognize contribution |
| Terminate employment | Support role transitions and new directions |
| Workforce planning | Community capability mapping |

---

### 7.1 Develop Human Development Strategy

#### L3 Processes
```
7.1.1  Develop community human development strategy
7.1.2  Design contribution pathways and civic participation models
7.1.3  Develop community capability mapping
7.1.4  Develop wellbeing and safety programs
7.1.5  Develop community workforce analytics (for institutional contributors)
```

#### L4 Activities
```
7.1.1.1  Align human development to institutional vision
7.1.1.2  Define what "contribution" means in post-labor context
7.1.2.1  Design multiple contribution pathways (stewardship, creative,
         civic, mentorship, environmental, care, research, etc.)
7.1.2.2  Ensure pathways are accessible across ability levels
7.1.3.1  Map community capabilities and interests
7.1.3.2  Identify capability development opportunities
7.1.4.1  Develop wellbeing support programs
7.1.4.2  Integrate with 15.0 (Human Flourishing)
```

---

### 7.2 Invite and Activate Contributors

#### L3 Processes
```
7.2.1  Identify institutional contribution needs
7.2.2  Invite and attract contributors (not "recruit")
7.2.3  Match contributors to roles based on interest and capability
7.2.4  Onboard contributors to institutional roles
```

## Implementation Guidance

### Phase 1: Constitutional Foundation (0–6 months)
Establish the institutional charter, governance structure, and foundational processes.
- Categories 1.0, 16.0, 11.0 are first-order priorities
- Define vision, democratic structure, and integrity framework before operations
- Establish Category 14.0 principles before deploying any AI systems

### Phase 2: Operating Core (6–18 months)
Stand up the civic operating processes that directly serve community members.
- Categories 2.0, 3.0, 5.0, 6.0 are the operating backbone
- Category 4.0 if physical distribution is in scope
- Category 15.0 integrated from the start (not bolted on later)

### Phase 3: Institutional Maturity (18–36 months)
Build the support processes that make operations sustainable and improvable.
- Categories 7.0, 8.0, 9.0, 10.0, 12.0, 13.0
- Mature Category 14.0 as AI systems scale
- Deepen Category 11.3 (equity monitoring) with data from operations

### Phase 4: Institutional Intelligence (36+ months)
Achieve adaptive, self-improving institutional operations.
- Move high-volume processes to Maturity Level 4–5
- Build feedback loops between all 16 categories
- Institutional AI governance becomes a model for other institutions

## Using This Standard

### For Designing a New PLE Institution
Walk through all 16 categories. For each L2 process group, ask: Does our
institution need this? If yes, who (or what) performs it? Document the answers.
The gaps are your design priorities.

### For Assessing an Existing Institution
Rate each applicable L2 process group on the maturity model (5 dimensions ×
5 levels). Color-code the results. The red zones are your transformation priorities.

### For Designing Inter-Institutional Coordination
Use the integration matrix to identify where Institution A's outputs feed
Institution B's inputs. Design the handoff protocols using the integration
design patterns (information flow, trigger flow, governance flow, feedback
loop, resource flow).

### For Human-AI Work Allocation
For each L4 activity, score Automation Potential (1–5) and AI Enhancement
Potential (1–5). Activities scoring 8+ are automation priorities. Activities
scoring 3–5 are human-primary. Activities in between are hybrid.

### For Democratic Accountability
Category 16.0 is not optional. Every other category operates *under* the
governance authority established in 16.0. Democratic legitimacy is the
foundation, not a feature.
